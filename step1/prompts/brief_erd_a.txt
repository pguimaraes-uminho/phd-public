A quick orientation on this airline data before you map it. The people in these records are travellers on flights - in aviation we call them passengers. Be a little wary of the word 'customer': in airline systems that can also mean a corporate account or travel agency that books but never flies, so when a table clearly holds the individual who actually travels, think of them as a passenger.

Mind the word 'flight'. A flight number such as AA100 names a scheduled service that repeats day after day; that same number on a particular date is one concrete operated flight, with its own aircraft, crew and outcome. When something is identified by flight number together with a date, it is really the dated, operated instance you are looking at, not the abstract schedule.

Every flight touches two airports that play opposite operational roles - the one it leaves and the one it reaches. They are never interchangeable: gates, customs, baggage handling and timings all differ by role. So if you see the same airport concept referenced more than once from a flight, expect genuinely different roles rather than an accidental duplicate.

Some associations in this domain carry facts of their own, and operations staff treat those as things in their own right, not as mere links. A seat allocation, for instance, is not just 'this person on this flight' - it records which seat, and the assignment itself is what people reason about. A flight's status is similar: it is something that happens at a moment in time, not a static label hanging off the flight.

Other associations really are just bridges with nothing of their own to record - for example, which crew members worked which flight. There is no extra data on the link itself; it is a plain many-to-many between two populations.

A flight's status value (departed, delayed, cancelled, on time, ...) is drawn from a small, controlled set that is reused across thousands of flights. It is worth separating the kind of status (the reusable classifier) from a particular status happening to a particular flight at a particular time (the occurrence).

An airport physically sits in a place - a city. Some designs keep that place as a concept of its own, others fold it into the airport. Either way the city is a locatable thing distinct from the airport facility that stands in it.

Columns that point at another table almost always encode a real-world relationship between things, not just a stored value: an airline operates a flight, a flight uses a baggage belt, a flight leaves one airport and reaches another, an aircraft flies a flight. It helps to read each such column as an active relationship and to ask which way it runs.

Finally, a note on language. Practitioners have settled, precise words for things, and the raw column names here are often terse codes or abbreviations rather than those words. When you name a concept or a property, reach for the term a controller, gate agent or dispatcher would actually use, and let a single packed column name unfold into the distinct facts it really carries.
